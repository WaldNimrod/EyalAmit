# EyalAmit.co.il — Marketing Messages and Copy

## The Core Positioning

Every word on the site is tested against one strategic question:

> **"Does this strengthen the understanding that Eyal Amit is an authority in breath treatment via didgeridoo?"**

This is the brand's center of gravity. Not "sound healing" (too broad). Not "didgeridoo teacher" (too narrow). Not "breathing exercises" (too generic). The specific, defensible position is: **טיפול בנשימה באמצעות דיג'רידו** — breath treatment via didgeridoo.

---

## What the Site Should NOT Sound Like

The site should NOT look or sound like:
- A general-purpose music school or instrument shop
- A hobbyist who plays for friends
- A generic wellness/spiritual page ("healing energies," "vibration medicine")
- A store front-and-center

The site SHOULD sound like:
- A professional treatment center with a named, developed method
- An authority — someone who has been doing this since 1999 and has developed a real approach
- A warm, accessible professional who speaks directly, uses humor where appropriate, and doesn't lecture
- A place where high-tech workers, doctors, managers, and business people also go — not just "seekers"

## The Personal Origin Story (Treatment Page)

This story is the emotional and credibility anchor of the site. It appears in full on the Treatment page. Key beats:

1. Eyal grew up in Givataim. At age 12, a fire killed a childhood friend and his entire family.
2. Complex family circumstances over the years.
3. Army service → asthma and allergies develop. Years on inhalers, steroids, alternative treatments that never truly resolved the problem.
4. Travels to India. In 2000, meets Mokesh Dahiman — "who became the greatest teacher of my life."
5. Breathwork through didgeridoo practice gradually heals the asthma and allergies completely.
6. The insight: the breath is not just a physical mechanism — it reflects and can be changed through active, structured practice.

**Why this story belongs on the site:** It demonstrates that Eyal's work comes from lived experience, not from a training course. It establishes the teacher-student lineage (Mokesh). It shows that the method works even for serious physical conditions (asthma → complete resolution). It makes the authority claim concrete and personal.

**Tone:** Direct, honest, no spiritual inflation. "Pulmonaut" (נאות-ריאה) is Eyal's own self-description — not a clinical term, used deliberately with self-awareness.

## The Distinction Block (Treatment vs. Sound Healing)

This is one of the most important content elements on the site — it appears both on the homepage and is developed fully on the Treatment page.

The confusion in the market: many people hear "didgeridoo treatment" and think it means passive sound healing (lying still while someone plays over them). The distinction:

| Treatment (טיפול בדיג'רידו) | Sound Healing (סאונד הילינג) |
|-----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Active — you play the instrument | Passive — you receive the sound |
| Individual session or small group | Group session |
| Breath is the subject | Relaxation / meditation is the purpose |
| Medical-adjacent application | Wellness/relaxation |
| Ongoing process | Single experience |
| cbDIDG method | Didgeridoo meditation |

The site consistently makes this distinction because blurring it creates confusion about what to expect from a session — and confused visitors don't book.

**Design note:** The disclaimer must be visually present but not dominant. It does not undermine the authority of the content — it contextualizes it correctly.

## Tone of Voice Summary

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Direct | Answers first, explanation after |
| Warm | First-person, human, not clinical |
| Credible | Cites research, names credentials, gives dates |
| Honest | Names what it is NOT, what it CAN'T do |
| Occasional humor | The 🙂 after "the breath belongs to everyone" — appropriate |
| No inflation | Avoids spiritual language that could alienate the skeptical audience |
| RTL-native | Written to be read right-to-left — sentence structures flow naturally in Hebrew |
